Why are statistics important for understanding elections?

Statistics are one of, if not the, only method(s) of objectively assessing election results, both before them and after they occur. Although statistics often make incorrect predictions, understanding the history of a given country and its politics can help you to understand much more about what may happen later in that country.

Most of the following statistcs are meant to illustrate the effects of disproportional or otherwise deficient electoral methods. The primary effect of disproportionate voting systems is to contribute to voter disengagement or exaggerate pre-existing or emerging disengagement trends. Conversely, electoral methods that are explicitly designed to be proportional and authentic to the needs and wants of the voting populace encourage turnout, helping to counteract voter disengagement and possibly even reverse previous trends of voter disengagement.

Turnout in German Federal Elections

Since its foundation, first as West Germany after World War II and continuing after reunification in 1989, Germany has used the mixed-member proportional voting system. Under this system, voters vote once for a constituency representative and then for a party list. Although each constituency only elects one constituency representative to the national legislature, every state elects several representatives to the national legislature off of the party list. Therefore, while regions retain the power thye hold in other first-past-the-post systems, proportionality is still allowed for and enforced by the party list. Turnout in German federal elections has never fallen below its lowest in 2009 at 70.8%. It is notable that in this election, the opposition party SPD lost 11.2% of their vote, while third parties like the FDP, the Left, and the Greens all received several percentage points more than the prior elections. Therefore, the drop in turnout can be accounted for by continued CDU/CSU dominance and a lack of opposition power.

Turnout in New Zealand General Elections

New Zealand uses an electoral system not dissimilar from that used by Germany. Under this system, voters still elected consituency representatives by first-past-the-post, but a further 55 seats were elected from party lists. In the first election using MMP, turnout spiked by 5.46% to reach a whopping 88.28%. Additionally, the number of Maori in Parliament nearly doubled, leading to "an all-time record of 17 MPs." Although New Zealand's turnout has fallen since, it has nonetheless never fallen below the 74.21% observed in the 2011 general election. Is worthy of note that in the 2011 general election, not only did the incumbent National Party increase their vote share, thus possibly accounting for the drop in turnout, but voters also decided to keep the MMP system. Even among those who did vote to scrap MMP and move to another voting system, only 46.7% voted to return to first-past-the-post.

Turnout in United Kingdom General Election

The United Kingdom's electoral system, a constituency-based first-past-the-post system is among the most disproportionate in the world. As will be shown below, the number of voters that each party must receive to gain MPs varies widely, especially among third parties that experience widespread support not concentrated in particular areas. While recently the Liberal Democrats have concentrated their power base among middle-class commuter constituencies, especially around London, the Conservatives and Labour continue to exert massive power upon the political system. Indeed, even the Scottish National Party, which once held nearly every seat in Scotland, has recently lost many of their seats in the 2024 general election, mostly to Labour with some others going to the Liberal Democrats and Conservatives. This system has disparaged parties like UKIP, Reform, and the Greens with widespread but unconcentrated support. In the 2015 general election, for example, UKIP received an astonishing 12.6% of the vote but receieved only a single seat in Parliament. In other words, they received 12.6% of the vote but only 0.2% of Parliamentary seats. Similarly, in the 2024 general election, Reform UK, the direct descendant of UKIP (down to being led by the same MP, Nigel Farage) received 12.3% of the vote but gained only 0.8% of the seats in Parliament. Meanwhile, Labour received 33.7% of the vote, representing a 1.6% change from their performance in 2019, but gained a staggering 63.2% of seats in Parliament.

Votes per MP in the 2015 United Kingdom General Election

As discussed earlier, the United Kingdom's first-past-the-post voting system is indescribably disproportionate. This is most directly and objectively reflected in the number of votes each party must receive to gain one MP in Parliament. As mentioned, UKIP received 12.6% of the vote---3,881,099 votes---but received only a single seat in Parliament. In other words, UKIP's 3,881,099 translated to only a single seat. By comparison, the Conservative's 11,229,609 seats translated to 330 seats; this means roughly 34,241 voters per seat. This means that 113 Conservative voters are worth one UKIP voter. If you think Cromwell succeeded, your head is a little too round.

Votes per MP in the 2024 United Kingdom General Election

A similar pattern to the 2015 general election can also be seen in the 2024 general election. As a result of the Conservative's perceived incompetenence and dissatisfaction with their performance after 14 continuous years of power, Labour received 63.2% of the seats in Parliament; however, their vote share turned out to be a mere 33.7% of the vote, underperforming polls by several points. Indeed, while the Conservativse did perform dismally, losing nearly 20% of their 2019 performance, only 1.6% of defecting Conservatives went to Labour. The vast majority of the Conservatives lost votes went to Reform, who, despite receiving 14.29% of the vote, representing a 12.3% swing from their 2019 performance, only received 5 seats.
